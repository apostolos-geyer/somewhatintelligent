# The somewhatintelligent brand theory

## The diagnosis

The current storefront is not ugly. It is worse: it is reasonable.

It has the familiar signs of technical taste—monospace labels, thin rules, cool gray, precise spacing, a pink accent—but they currently function as proof of restraint rather than as a point of view. The page says “a competent developer made a store.” The shirt says something far stranger and more valuable: desire can hide inside a technical specification, and the specification can be produced as evidence after the joke has already implicated you.

That movement is the brand:

1. **A blunt human claim.** “I think we should be friends.”
2. **A technical breach.** “In the C++ way.”
3. **Documentary evidence.** The entire `friend` declaration reference, printed with absurd seriousness.

The website should use the same three-beat structure at every scale. Seduce first. Complicate second. Prove last.

## The core thesis

**somewhatintelligent is a publishing house for semiotic goods.**

It publishes four media:

- **Objects** — clothing and physical releases.
- **Systems** — software and subscriptions.
- **Texts** — essays, notes, arguments, and documentation.
- **The person** — Apostoli as builder, writer, speaker, and operator; present as author, never advertised as labor.

These are not four unrelated business lines. They are four ways of making an idea executable. A shirt can carry a language-law into public space. A SaaS product can turn an opinion about infrastructure into behavior. An essay can expose the ideology hidden inside an interface. A person can be hired as the unstable runtime connecting all three.

The website is therefore neither portfolio nor ecommerce site nor SaaS holding company. It is the **public interface of a private system**.

## The contradiction to preserve

The work repeatedly returns to systems that promise freedom while shaping what freedom can mean: platforms, protocols, algorithms, institutions, ethical codes, and infrastructure. It also repeatedly punctures its own authority with the body, sex, an ex, pornography, a bad joke, or a sentence that admits the author is inside the system he is criticizing.

Do not resolve that contradiction. Institutional confidence without leakage becomes corporate-power cosplay. Leakage without discipline becomes a meme account. The brand lives in the failure of each to fully contain the other.

The desired ratio is:

> **70% apparatus / 20% body / 10% embarrassment**

The embarrassment matters. It proves a person is still present.

## Positioning

### What it is

- A small institution with one author and too many departments.
- An editorial project that happens to sell usable things.
- A software studio that admits software is cultural production.
- A fashion label whose references compile.
- A personal site that refuses the sanitized performance of employability.

### What it is not

- Developer merch with syntax highlighting pasted on it.
- A brutalist grid full of fake accession numbers.
- A terminal theme.
- “Cyberpunk.”
- A meme store.
- A theory moodboard that forces every joke to explain Lacan.
- A founder cult pretending to be a research institute.

## The name

`somewhatintelligent` is excellent because it sabotages the authority it creates.

It is lowercase, overlong, difficult to typeset neatly, and inherently provisional. It can introduce an institution, a person, or a diagnosis. It does not need a symbol urgently. The word itself is the mark.

Keep it lowercase in ordinary brand use. Allow `SOMEWHATINTELLIGENT` only when the voice is explicitly institutional: legal lines, garment labels, shipping marks, build metadata. Never shorten the public-facing wordmark to `SI`; the abbreviation loses the joke and acquires the visual vocabulary of a consultancy.

## The visual world: the intimate apparatus

Treat garments as designed equipment: versioned releases accumulate into a coherent system, product pages describe construction as a concept, and even patches belong to a larger body of work. The seriousness comes from the objects and their documentation, not borrowed corporate mythology.

The somewhatintelligent version is an **intimate apparatus**:

- corporate evidence photography contaminated by flash, sweat, skin, handwriting, and bedroom-scale proximity;
- technical documentation interrupted by sentences that are too personal for the document;
- expensive restraint punctured once per surface by a vulgar or vulnerable truth;
- product images that alternate between forensic object study and a body actually wearing the thing;
- interface structures that reveal what something is connected to, not decorative diagrams pretending connection.

### Signature gesture: the private leak

Every major surface gets one controlled breach where private material crosses into public structure.

Examples:

- A hover on `private` reveals a handwritten “you can access mine.”
- A product specification includes “social behavior: non-transitive.”
- A release note quietly links to the essay or video that produced the object.
- The homepage’s perfect institutional masthead is crossed by one oversized, off-register sentence: **I MAKE THINGS I CAN’T EXPLAIN WITHOUT MAKING THEM WORSE.**
- An About record borrows the grammar of an employee credential, then refuses the transaction: **APOSTOLI / NOT FOR HIRE.**

Use one leak, not twelve. Repetition turns transgression into wallpaper.

## Color

The current pink is worth keeping because it belongs to the first product. It should stop behaving like a generic primary button color and become **semantic evidence**: the color of `friend`, annotations, exposed private state, and active desire.

| Token        | Hex       | Use                                                        |
| ------------ | --------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| `void`       | `#08090A` | Primary ground, black garments, image fields               |
| `cold-paper` | `#DDE0DE` | Light ground; deliberately gray-green, not lifestyle cream |
| `oxide`      | `#8C918D` | Rules, metadata, disabled matter                           |
| `friend`     | `#FF4FA3` | The breach: keywords, links, selected state, annotations   |
| `bruise`     | `#6C63FF` | Rare software/system state; never a gradient               |
| `error`      | `#E43B32` | Actual destructive or contradictory state                  |

Black and cold paper should carry nearly everything. Pink should occupy less than five percent of a normal page and much more of a single campaign moment. Bruise belongs primarily to software, giving the SaaS family a distinct but related signal.

No ambient gradients. No glass. No soft “premium” shadows. If depth is needed, use physical overlap, sharp crop, taped paper, browser windows, garment folds, and actual light.

## Typography

The present all-Iosevka system makes every sentence sound like interface copy. Keep Iosevka, but demote it from personality to evidence.

Three voices are needed:

1. **Institutional grotesk** — mastheads, commerce, navigation, blunt claims. It should be narrow enough to set the long name as an object. Directional references: Söhne Schmal, Univers Condensed, or a similarly severe grotesk.
2. **Humanist text serif** — essays, product concepts, intimate annotations. It should feel printed and intelligent without performing old-world luxury. Directional references: Tiempos Text, Lyon Text, or a sturdy contemporary editorial serif.
3. **Iosevka** — code, IDs, release data, measurements, receipts, errors, and documentary material.

Open-source implementation route: Archivo Narrow + Source Serif 4 + Iosevka. Commercial art-direction route: Söhne Schmal + Tiempos Text + Iosevka.

The memorable typographic move is not “big type.” It is the collision between an enormous condensed statement and a small, patient paragraph that disproves or embarrasses it.

## Image theory

The image system has three modes. Every release should use all three.

### 1. Evidence

Front-lit, clinical, nearly shadowless object records: garment flat, print detail, label, fabric, packaging, receipt. Hard crops. Neutral surface. Nothing aspirational.

### 2. Incident

Direct-flash documentary images where something appears to have just happened: elevator, parking structure, office corridor at night, unmade bed, server room, lecture hall after everyone left. Subjects are close enough to implicate the camera. The garment is worn, not presented.

### 3. Contamination

Scans, fingerprints, redactions, screenshots, diffs, reference pages, handwriting, thermal artifacts, imperfect halftones. These are not decorative textures. Each must be traceable to the idea or making of the object.

Do not use faceless “techwear” models standing in blue fog. Do not use random surveillance overlays, coordinates, or crosshairs. A real receipt is more convincing than a fake dossier.

## Product architecture

Use identifiers only where they encode something real:

```text
OBJECT / FRIEND-001
SYSTEM / INBOX-001
TEXT / CYBER-OTHER
PERSON / APOSTOLI
```

Physical goods and software share **semantic versioned releases**. Texts keep dates and revisions. The person has authorship and boundaries. Each medium keeps honest state while the public archive gains one coherent release grammar.

An object page should connect:

```text
idea → incident/video → object → specification → related text/system
```

This is the key UX advantage over a conventional shop: the visitor can see not only what the object costs, but what caused it to exist.

## Mechanisms worth borrowing

- **From institutional merchandise programs:** versioned releases, exact material descriptions, object-level seriousness, and a product ecosystem. Reject the militarized aura and corporate self-mythology.
- **From 032c:** media and fashion as one company rather than “content marketing” attached to clothes; every output can inform, provoke, or activate.
- **From Brain Dead:** a brand can interpret culture through clothes, objects, and artworks without asking one category to subordinate itself to another.
- **From Are.na:** collections and connections are themselves an interface; let people traverse the relations among ideas instead of consuming a chronological feed.
- **From MACK:** rigor and material care make critical thought desirable without turning it into empty luxury.

The synthesis is specific: **publish ideas in whatever medium makes them most dangerous.**

## The test

Before anything ships, ask:

1. Does it make a clear claim before it explains itself?
2. Is the technical material doing conceptual work, or merely signaling “developer”?
3. Is there evidence that a person made this?
4. Is there one breach of the system, and only one?
5. Could this belong to another tasteful technical founder?

If the answer to the fifth question is yes, it is not done.
